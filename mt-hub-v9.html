Mindset and Psychology
Why free value first changes everything ▼
Most outreach fails because it leads with a pitch. When you open with a genuine GMB observation — something specific to their business — you are already different from every other agency that has ever messaged them. The free audit is not a hook. It is the actual value. Trades respect straight talk and practical help. That is what this is.
The psychology of the trade business owner ▼
A trades business owner is on the tools all day, quoting in evenings, managing the crew. Marketing is last on their list. They are also sceptical — burned before by agencies who took money and delivered nothing. Your job is to be the opposite: short messages, no jargon, real value. Speak to what keeps them up at night — not enough leads, unreliable pipeline, slow months.
Volume, consistency and why rejection is not failure ▼
A 10 to 15 percent reply rate is good. A 5 percent rate is not failure — it is an invitation to refine. The setters who scale treat it like a numbers game with a human touch. Send 100 messages. Get 10 to 15 replies. Book 3 to 5 audit calls. Close 1 to 2. That is the funnel. Volume creates the pipeline. Consistency fills it. The best day to send is the day you do not feel like it.
Why reviews are the most powerful asset a trade has ▼
When someone searches plumber near me and sees one result with 4 reviews and another with 47 five-star reviews, the decision is already made before they click. Reviews are proof at the point of decision. They also tell Google you are trusted — which improves ranking, visibility, and enquiries. It compounds. Most trades do brilliant work but never ask for a review. That is five more five-star reviews sitting unclaimed after every job.

Setter Rules
Read these before you send a single message
Van Time Strategy

Always suggest the call happens while they are driving. Trades view driving as dead time — a 5-minute call while moving feels free, not like a commitment.

Lower-Case Energy

No capitals. No formal greetings. Use lowercase "i". No perfect punctuation. It proves it is a person typing, not a bot. Formal messages get ignored.

"Sort It Yourself" Anchor

The phrase "sort it yourself" is your shield. It removes the "what is the catch" fear before they even think it. Use it in every message.

Speed to Lead

Reply within 5 minutes of any response. Once they get back on the tools you have lost them. If you cannot reply fast, do not send outreach you cannot service.

Point, Do Not Pitch

You are not selling an audit. You found a wide-open front door and you are pointing at it. The moment it sounds like a pitch, it is over. Be the guy who noticed.

One Ask Then Stop

Every message ends with one question. After you ask it, stop typing. If you ask and then fill the silence yourself you have just answered your own objection for them.

GMB Monthly Management — What to Do Every Month
The exact tasks that keep a profile ranking and converting
1
Week 1 — Post to the profile
Publish at least one GMB post. Options: completed job with a photo, a seasonal service reminder, or a quick tip for homeowners. Include the trade and location in the post text. Photos get significantly more engagement than text-only posts.
2
Week 2 — Respond to all new reviews
Check for any new reviews. Respond to every single one — positive and negative. For positive: thank them by name, mention the job type if possible, keep it warm. For negative: acknowledge, do not argue, offer to resolve offline. Google notices response rate.
3
Week 3 — Photo check
Upload at least one new photo of completed work if available from the client. Ask the client to send a photo after any significant job this month. Name the file descriptively before uploading (e.g. manchester-boiler-installation.jpg).
4
Week 4 — Profile health check
Check: has anything changed? Are opening hours still correct? Is the phone number right? Has anything in the services list changed? Google occasionally auto-suggests edits — check the profile for any suggested changes and approve or reject them.
5
End of month — compile the report
Go to Tools → GMB Report Generator. Fill in the stats for the month. Send to the client. The report shows them what you have done and what it is producing. This is what keeps clients on the monthly fee — they can see it working.

Setter and Sales Training
Module 1 — Understanding What You Are Actually Selling ▼
GMB is the most underused free tool in a trades business. When someone searches plumber near me, Google shows three results first — the local 3-pack. Getting into that is worth thousands per month. Complete profile plus regular posts plus strong reviews plus website link equals ranking. We handle all of it. The more you understand what you are doing for these businesses, the easier it is to talk about it like it matters — because it does.
Module 2 — The Opener: What Works and Why ▼
The opener is an observation and an offer. The psychological principle is reciprocity — when you give something useful for free, people feel a natural pull to engage. Specificity is the key. Saying your GMB could do better fails. Saying you are showing up just not in a couple of the spots you should be lands because it is true and specific. Every opener: specific observation, offer of free value, question that keeps it open. No pitch. No price. No pressure.
Module 3 — Reading Replies and Qualifying ▼
Three types of reply: interested and ready — push to book. Interested but cautious — give more value, answer questions, then book. Not interested — respect it, ask why, archive. The fastest way to destroy a lead is to pitch before they are warm. The fastest way to miss a sale is to keep giving value without asking for the call. Answer their questions then redirect: makes sense to get that 15 minute call booked — what does your week look like?
Module 4 — Objection Handling ▼
Objections are questions in disguise. Too expensive means help me see the value. I will think about it means I am not convinced yet. Already have someone means prove you are different. The rule in this business: one sentence response then back to the ask, then stop talking and let them respond. See the Scripts section for every objection handled exactly. Do not improvise — use what is there.
Module 5 — Booking the Call ▼
Your job ends at the booked call. Your finish line is a specific day and time with a Zoom link sent. Not sometime next week. Not let me know when you are free. When someone says yes to a call, go straight to availability. Do not give them time to change their mind. Confirm immediately. Send the link immediately. Remind the evening before. If they do not show, one follow-up only.
Module 6 — Reading Tone and Body Language in Messages ▼
Messages do not come with tone of voice — but they have signals. Short replies usually mean low interest or high distraction. Detailed replies usually mean genuine interest. Questions about cost early mean they are thinking seriously but have a concern. "I will let you know" usually means no — but asking what specifically they want to think through can unlock the real objection. The single most useful skill in text-based selling is learning to read what someone is not saying. If a message feels off, it probably is. Address the vibe, not just the words: "I get the sense you might be on the fence — is there something I have not covered properly?"
Module 7 — Handling Silence and Ghosting ▼
Most setters give up after one follow-up. Most sales happen after the third or fourth contact. The difference between a closer and a setter who quits is persistence without desperation. The rule: three touches after a positive response, then one exit message, then long-term nurture. Do not apologise for following up. Do not say "sorry to bother you again." Say "just circling back on this" — you are doing your job, not bothering someone. Silence is not rejection. Silence is usually busyness, distraction, or waiting for the right moment. Your job is to make the right moment easier to say yes in.
Module 8 — Pricing Psychology ▼
Never apologise for the price. Never justify it before someone has objected to it. Lead with value, not cost. The sequence that works: show the problem, show the opportunity, show the solution, then mention the price. If someone objects to the price, the fix is almost never reducing it — it is increasing the perceived value. Anchor high: "Some agencies charge £500 a month for this and deliver nothing — we charge £147 and you can see exactly what we do." The number that matters is not the price — it is the price compared to the opportunity cost of doing nothing. "£147 a month to manage the thing that could bring you £2,000 a month in extra work" is not expensive. It is obvious.
Module 9 — Finding the Best Leads ▼
The best leads are trades businesses that are already trying but getting it wrong. They have a GMB but it is incomplete. They have a few reviews but stopped asking. They have a website but it does not rank. They are close — they just need the last 20%. Businesses with no profile at all are harder — they have decided not to prioritise Google and need more convincing. Look for: 5 to 20 reviews, incomplete description, no photos or very few, not showing in the local 3-pack despite clearly operating in the area. Search "[trade] in [location]" and look at who is on page 2. Those are your warmest leads — they are not invisible, they are just not in the right place.
Module 10 — Writing a Personalised Opener from Scratch ▼
The templates work. But occasionally a prospect needs something more specific. The formula for a personalised opener: observation + specific detail + free value offer + one question. The observation must be true and specific — not generic. "Your profile could do better" fails. "Your profile has 4 reviews and no photos of your work, which means Google is probably showing you to fewer people than it should" works. The specific detail shows you have actually looked. The free value offer removes the feeling of being sold to. The question keeps it open. Write it, read it back, and ask: would this feel like a personal message or like a template? If it feels like a template, make one more thing specific.

Running the Business
Module 1 — Managing vs Leading ▼
Managing is making sure tasks get done. Leading is making sure the right people want to do them. This hub makes managing almost automatic — the SOPs, checklists, scripts — so your energy goes into leading. Setting the tone, giving feedback, raising the standard. When Cam is running it while you are in surgery, the hub manages. You lead by having built it properly.
Module 2 — Giving Feedback That Improves Things ▼
Good feedback is specific, timely and forward-facing. Not that message was bad — that message did not have a specific observation. Next time, use the opener script exactly. Here is why it lands better. Always close feedback with what a good version looks like. Giving someone a problem without a direction is demoralising.
Module 3 — Running the Zoom Close ▼
Never go in with a pitch. Go in with their GMB pulled up on screen, show them what you can see, explain what it means in plain terms, let the gap between where they are and where they could be do the selling. Then ask: based on what we have looked at today, do you want us to handle this for you? Then stop talking. The best closes are the ones where the prospect almost asks you first.
Module 4 — Spotting Struggle in Your Team ▼
People rarely say when they are struggling. Signals: reply rates drop, energy changes, excuses about the leads. When you spot it, do not wait. Have a straight conversation: I have noticed things feel harder this week — what is going on? Most of the time they are stuck on something specific you can fix in ten minutes.
Module 5 — Delegation and Letting Go ▼
The business cannot scale if everything runs through you. Every task only you can do is a ceiling. Delegation is not dumping tasks — it is handing over ownership with context, standards, and trust. Give the full picture, show the standard, then let them own it. Check in without hovering. The measure of good delegation: things get done well when you are not watching.
Module 6 — What Good GMB Management Actually Looks Like Week to Week ▼
Most clients think GMB management means setting it up and leaving it. The reason it compounds is consistency. Google's algorithm rewards regular activity — posts, new photos, review responses, profile updates. A profile managed well for 6 months outperforms a profile set up perfectly once. Your job as the person running delivery is to make this a habit, not a task. The monthly management SOP tells you exactly what to do each week. The monthly report shows the client it is working. The review card creates the reviews that fuel the ranking. These three things together — activity, evidence, social proof — is the product. It is not just the setup.
Module 7 — Running a Discovery Call vs a Close Call ▼
A discovery call is where you find out what the client actually needs. A close call is where you present the solution and ask for the decision. The Zoom audit call is both — but most people treat it like only one. The discovery part: listen to what they tell you before you show them anything. What do they think is wrong? What have they tried? What does success look like to them? Then the close: show them the audit, show them the gap between where they are and where they could be, make one recommendation. If you skip the discovery, the close feels like a pitch. If you do the discovery properly, the close almost happens on its own.
Module 8 — Team Performance — What to Measure and When to Act ▼
The metrics that matter for a setter: messages sent, reply rate, calls booked. The metrics that matter for a closer: calls taken, conversion rate, average deal value. Reply rate below 10% for more than a week means the openers need work — look at the specific messages that are getting no response. Calls booked but not converting means the leads are not being qualified properly before booking — the setter is booking anyone. A good conversion rate on the Zoom call is 30% or above. Below that, either the audit is not landing, the objection handling is weak, or the leads are not warm enough. Check the tracker weekly. The numbers tell you what to fix before it becomes a problem.
Module 9 — Scaling from One Setter to a Full Team ▼
The biggest mistake when scaling is adding people before the process is solid. If one setter is inconsistent with the scripts, two setters will be twice as inconsistent. Get the process right with one person, document every edge case, then duplicate it. When you hire a second setter, the onboarding is already in this hub — the AI trainer, the week 1 plan, the scripts, the checklists. They should not need you in the room. The test of whether the process is ready to scale: can Cam run it without asking you a single question? If yes, it is ready. If not, the gap is in the documentation, not the person.
Module 10 — Building Systems That Run Without You ▼
This hub is the start of the answer to that question. Every SOP, script, checklist, and training module is a piece of the business that no longer needs to live in your head. The goal is a business that could run for two weeks without you and you would come back to find it in the same or better shape. You are not there yet — but the surgery test is a good one. Would Cam know what to do? Would a new hire know what to do? If the answer is no to either, something needs to be added to the hub. Think of it as your second-in-command that never sleeps, never forgets, and never needs to ask you the same question twice.

Why Reviews Win Jobs Before a Word Is Spoken
Why reviews win jobs before a word is spoken ▼
When a homeowner searches for a trade on Google, the decision is made in seconds. A business with 47 reviews and 4.9 stars beats a business with 4 reviews every single time. Reviews are proof at the point of decision. They also tell Google you are trusted — which improves ranking, visibility, and enquiries. It compounds. Most trades do brilliant work but never ask for a review.
When to ask — and when not to ▼
Best moment: right after the job is finished, when the client is happy. Not a week later. In person: I would really appreciate it if you left us a Google review — here is a card, takes two minutes. Second best: the day after via WhatsApp. When NOT to ask: if there was any unresolved issue during the job. Deal with that first.
What kills your review rate ▼
Asking too late: Within 24 hours is the window.

Too much friction: If they have to find the business on Google themselves, most will not. The review card removes all friction.

Only asking once: A friendly follow-up 3–5 days later is fine — people intend to leave reviews and forget.

Bulk requests: Google flags sudden spikes. One or two a week is the natural pace.

Incentivising: Google explicitly prohibits this. Never offer anything in exchange.
Responding to reviews — why it matters ▼
Responding to every review is a Google ranking signal and a trust signal to anyone reading. For positive: thank them personally, mention the job type if possible. For negative: never argue, never get defensive. Acknowledge, apologise if warranted, offer to resolve offline. One professionally handled negative review can increase trust more than ten positives — it shows a business that takes responsibility.

Competitor Research Guide
How to find what others in the area are doing and use it in the audit
Step 1 — Find who is ranking above them ▼
Open an incognito browser. Search "[trade] in [location]" — e.g. "plumber in Manchester." The businesses in the top 3 of the map pack are the ones to study. Note their names. These are the competitors you will reference on the call.
Step 2 — Compare their review count and rating ▼
Look at the review count and average rating for the top 3. If the prospect has 8 reviews and the top result has 94, that contrast is your most powerful talking point. On the call: "The business showing above you has 94 reviews. You have 8. That is the gap we are looking at." One sentence.
Step 3 — Check their profile completeness ▼
Click into one of the top competitors. Check: do they have a description? Multiple photos? Services listed? Recent posts? Often the top-ranking business is not doing everything right — they are just doing more than the prospect. The message: "You do not need to be perfect, you need to be better than what is currently showing up."
Step 4 — Check their website on mobile ▼
Open the competitor website on your phone. Is it fast? Is there a phone number visible immediately? A call to action? Sometimes the top-ranking business has a mediocre website — that is an opportunity: "The business above you has a site that loads slowly. If we build you something better, you take that advantage."
Step 5 — Look for gaps even the top competitors have ▼
Even top-ranking businesses often have gaps — no Q&A answered, no posts in 3 months, reviews not responded to. These are opportunities. "Even the business currently ranking above you has not done X. If we do that for you, there is a clear path to overtaking them."

GMB Posting Guide
The 4-week rotation and posting rules
What GMB posts actually do for your ranking ▼
Google uses post activity as a signal that the business is active. Businesses that post weekly rank higher than those that do not. A fresh post from this week tells anyone who finds you that the business is alive and working.
The 4-week rotation that works ▼
Week 1: Tip — a genuinely useful piece of advice for homeowners. Week 2: Social proof — share a recent 5-star review with job type and area. Week 3: Job showcase — completed work with photo. Week 4: Offer or local area — availability, seasonal offer, or naming a specific town. Repeat.
Caption rules ▼
Start with the most important line. Include the area at least once. Include the trade type. End with a call to action. Do not keyword stuff. Do not post the same caption twice.
How to post on GMB step by step ▼
1. Search your business name in Google while logged in. 2. Click Add Update. 3. Choose What is New. 4. Upload the image from the Post Factory. 5. Paste the Body_Text from the CSV as the caption. 6. Add a CTA button — Call or Book. 7. Publish.
